BIODIVERSITY OF MICROORGANISMS

2.1 Introduction

Some living organisms are so small that they cannot be seen with the naked eye and are known as microorganisms e.g. viruses, bacteria, protists and fungi.

2.1.1 Viruses

Viruses are very small, acellular organisms with no nucleus, cytoplasm or organelles. A virus consists of nucleic acid (DNA or RNA ‚Äì not both) surrounded by a protein capsule. Viruses do not perform metabolic reactions and reproduce only inside living cells. They can be rod-shaped, spherical or complex in shape and are disease-causing pathogens.

Bacteria

Bacteria are unicellular prokaryotic organisms classified under Kingdom Monera. They lack membrane-bound organelles. The cell contains a cell wall, plasma membrane, cytoplasm and genetic material concentrated in a nucleoid. Some have a slime capsule and move using flagella. Most reproduce asexually through binary fission. Shapes include bacillus (rod), coccus (spherical), spirillum (spiral) and vibrio (comma-shaped). Some are autotrophic but most are heterotrophic.

Protista

  • Protists are unicellular (e.g. Amoeba) but some are multicellular (e.g. algae).
  • They are eukaryotes and have a true nucleus.
  • Algae are autotrophic and photosynthesize because they have chloroplasts.
  • Some protists (e.g. Amoeba) are heterotrophic.
  • Locomotory structures include pseudopodia (Amoeba), cilia (Paramecium) and flagella (Euglena).
  • Reproduction is mostly asexual (binary fission) but some reproduce sexually (e.g. some algae).

Fungi

  • Some fungi are unicellular (yeasts) and some multicellular (mushrooms, moulds).
  • All fungi are eukaryotic with true nuclei.
  • Cell walls consist of chitin.
  • Fungi are heterotrophic and have no chloroplasts.
  • Most are saprophytes (e.g. bread mould, mushrooms).
  • Some are parasites.
  • Multicellular fungi consist of hyphae forming a mycelium.
  • Reproduce sexually and asexually.
  • Yeasts reproduce by budding.
  • Multicellular fungi reproduce asexually by spores.

2.2 Roles in Maintaining Environmental Balance

Producers in Food Chains

  • Autotrophic bacteria and algae produce organic nutrients via photosynthesis.
  • Chemosynthetic bacteria use chemical reactions instead of sunlight.
  • Energy stored in carbohydrates is passed to consumers.

Maintaining Oxygen and Carbon Dioxide Levels

  • Autotrophic bacteria and algae use carbon dioxide and release oxygen.
  • This maintains atmospheric gas balance.

Role as Decomposers

  • Decomposition bacteria, saprophytic fungi and protists break down dead matter.
  • Release water, carbon dioxide, ammonia and energy.
  • Recycle elements like carbon, nitrogen and phosphorus.

Role in the Nitrogen Cycle

  • Bacteria recycle nitrogen.
  • Atmospheric nitrogen converted to nitrates by lightning and nitrogen-fixing bacteria.
  • Plants absorb nitrates; animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants.
  • Decomposition bacteria break down proteins into ammonia.
  • Nitrifying bacteria convert ammonia to nitrates.
  • Denitrifying bacteria convert nitrates back to atmospheric nitrogen.

2.3 Symbiotic Relationships

Nitrogen-Fixing Bacteria in Plants

  • Nitrogen forms part of proteins.
  • Atmospheric nitrogen cannot be used directly by most organisms.
  • Mutualistic nodule bacteria on legume roots convert nitrogen gas into usable nitrates.
